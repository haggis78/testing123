Palaeography Samples

One of the more challenging parts of working with medieval and early modern documents is that their handwriting differed from ours, sometimes significantly. At first, the novice palaeographer may well look at a sheet of illegible chickenscratch and despair of ever being able to read it. But with a little practice, you will find that everything starts to fall into place.

Naturally, it is more challenging if the scribe is writing unfamiliar words, or unfamiliar spellings of familiar words, or above all, in an altogether unfamiliar language.

In this case, we are looking at a Latin document written in 1685. To make matters worse, this manuscript was damaged by water in the 1720s or 1730s, leading some of the pages to become translucent or blotchy. Nonetheless, this image is from a clear part of the text. As you will see, the scribe's handwriting, while somewhat unfamiliar, is at least clear and consistent -- and in fact rather attractive without being over the top.

Begin reading at the bold capital S in the fourth line.

  1. Statuit dictus Ric[ard]us Menevensis Ep[iscop]us Capitulo Suo con-
  2. -sensum praebende quod quilibet Vicarioru[m} in Eccl[es]ia Menevensi resi-
  3. -dentiu[m] singulis diebus intersint horis quatuor Canonicis, vid[e]l[ice]t, matu-
  4. -tinis, horae primae, et magnae missae et versperis. Qui autem in aliquo
  5. de praemissis horis absens fuerit absq[ue] licentia illius qui praeest,
  6. vel sine rationabili causa unu[m] denariu[m] de sua com[m]una seu
  7. portione amittat, et ille denarius praesentibus Vicarijs assignetur.

You will note that there are several abbreviations in this text. Medieval handwriting, especially Latin, tended to be far more heavily abbreviated, but at least its abbreviations were highly standardized. Even here, in a post-medieval hand, the abbreviations are fairly consistent: Ep~us will always mean Episcopus (bishop), and a line over a letter usually means that an m or an n should be inserted after that letter. To learn more, see Charles Trice Martin's book The Record Interpreter.

For further practice, you might want to check out the excellent palaeography resources site from The National Archives (the British one, not the American).
